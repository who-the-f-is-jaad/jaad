The Literary Score: Musical Perception and Interior Audition in Proust

Excerpt from a Master's dissertation — Paris-Sorbonne, supervised by Denis Labouret

Reading Marcel Proust's descriptions of the Vinteuil Sonata in In Search of Lost Time, one encounters a peculiar phenomenon: the prose seems not merely to describe music but to produce it. Why, confronted with a fictional sonata by a fictional composer, does the reader come away with something resembling the experience of having heard it?

My central argument is that Proust constructs what I call a literary score — a phenomenological mode of writing that generates, in the act of reading, an interior audition structurally analogous to that experienced by a musician sight-reading a piece of music. To make this case, the research moves from the physics of sound to the phenomenology of listening, and finally to the poetics of Proustian prose itself.

I

Sound as a perceptual event

Any account of Proust's musical prose must begin with what a sound actually is. The standard acoustic definition — a vibration propagating through an elastic medium — is insufficient because it brackets out precisely what matters: the perceiving subject. A tree falls in a forest; without a listener, there is vibration, but no sound. Husserl's phenomenology offers the corrective: the perceived object is never fully possessed by the subject but constituted through Abschattungen — profiles or sketches that the perceiving consciousness assembles into a whole.

Proust's writing enacts exactly this structure. A note on a stringed instrument is not a single event but a cascade of harmonics unfolding sequentially in time — each a secondary vibration following the fundamental tone. The ear does not receive a discrete datum but reconstructs a unity from multiplicity across time. This is the model of perception — active, temporal, constitutive — that Proust's prose internalizes.

The impossibility of silence in La Recherche follows directly from this insight. Silence, for Proust, is never simply the absence of sound; it is always a perceived absence — a consciousness braced for sound, projecting its own interior noise into the gap. In the bedroom at Doncières, the narrator barely achieves a moment of quiet before a watch-tick, a footstep, the crumbling of a cinder reassert the world. The auditory subject cannot close itself off as the visual subject can behind its eyelids. Hearing is constitutively open.

II

Swann listens badly

One of La Recherche's subtlest arguments is that most listening is a form of failure — and its primary exhibit is Charles Swann. Swann's celebrated responses to the Vinteuil Sonata are, for Proust, a case study in aesthetic underperformance. He projects onto the music the texture of his love for Odette: the little phrase becomes the anthem of an affair, a confidente rather than an autonomous aesthetic object. Swann is lucid enough to recognize this — he knows he “is reasoning not about the phrase itself but about mere surrogates substituted for his intelligence's convenience” — yet he cannot overcome it.

This distinction matters, because Swann is not the narrator. To conflate them — as Proust criticism frequently does — is to mistake the novel's diagnosis for its cure. Where Swann's listening remains semi-passive, a reverie triggered by music rather than an engagement with it, the narrator moves toward genuinely musical attention: one that subordinates the ego's projections to the work's own temporality. Paradoxically, it is not Swann's ignorance of music theory that disqualifies him — the narrator is equally a non-musician, and his ignorance opens him to “impressions that are perhaps the only purely musical ones, entirely original, irreducible to any other order of impression.” What disqualifies Swann is self-absorption, not ignorance.

III

The literary score and interior audition

If Proust's subject is musical perception, his method is to reproduce that perception in the reader. This is the point at which his project exceeds what musicologists call mélophrasis — the verbal evocation of music. Mélophrasis describes; Proust performs.

The vehicle is the narrator's focalisation. Plunged into Swann's stream of consciousness during the salon performance, the reader does not receive a description of the Sonata but experiences, alongside Swann, the phenomenology of listening itself: the momentary lostness before the phrase crystallizes, the effort of reconstruction, the sensation that the phrase withdraws even as it is grasped. Proust's famously long sentences — with their self-correcting qualifications, parentheses that open and never quite close — mime the temporal arc of musical apprehension. When Swann “tried to hold onto the phrase or the harmony — he did not know himself — which had opened his soul more widely,” the syntactic hesitation is not vagueness. It is the structural trace of a perception that exceeds language at the very moment of its occurrence.

The result is interior audition: a reading experience in which the prose itself functions as a score, activating the reader's imaginative hearing in the same way that a printed musical score activates the inner ear of a trained musician.

The fiction of Vinteuil's music works not because Proust describes its qualities convincingly, but because he inscribes the structure of listening into the syntax of his sentences. His deepest claim for literature is that it can do what music cannot articulate and what music cannot narrate — occupying the interval between them, a total art.
