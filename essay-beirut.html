How Beirut's Post-Rock and Shoegaze scene builds in the rubble

From a free studio in Sin el-Fil to a skatepark in Horsh Beirut, a small network of musicians keeps building.

Last October, Bliss hauled their amps and gear into the middle of Snoubar Skatepark in Horsh Beirut, set up a stage, and played a full set while people dropped into ramps all around them. No venue, no booking fee, no production budget. Just a three-piece band from Beirut and a crowd that showed up because they wanted to be there. Julie Abou Kasm, the band's bassist and vocalist, calls it the best concert they've ever played.

It's a small story, but it tells you something about how this scene works. Lebanon's post-rock and shoegaze community doesn't rely on infrastructure, because infrastructure in Lebanon tends to disappear. What it has instead is a tight web of people who find a way.

I

The guitar-driven, texture-first music coming out of Beirut today didn't arrive out of nowhere. It grew from an improv scene that spent years playing to rooms that laughed at them.

Mazen Kerbaj and Sharif Sehnaoui met as teenagers in Beirut, bonded over psychedelic and prog records, and started building a small network of players in the late 1990s. Sehnaoui went to Paris, found the free-improv community at Les Instants Chavirés, and came back with a prepared-guitar technique. Kerbaj taught himself trumpet from a horn Sehnaoui handed him and developed his own vocabulary of what he called "small sounds." Between 1998 and 2003, by Sehnaoui's account, audiences at their shows would basically laugh at almost everything they did.

In 2000, the two founded Irtijal — Arabic for "improvisation" — now the oldest continuously running music festival in Beirut. It is the institution that made experimental and post-rock music thinkable here, and it still runs every year. Kerbaj started the label Al Maslakh in 2005 to release what nobody else would touch. The physical hub was La CD-Thèque, a record shop run by Tony Sfeir, which put out a 2001 compilation called Beirut Incognito — the disc that first got Kerbaj's solo trumpet in front of customers, some of whom returned it thinking the CD was broken.

Then came the summer of 2006. On the night of July 15th, during Israel's 33-day war on Lebanon, Kerbaj sat on his balcony and improvised on trumpet against the sound of bombs falling on the city. The recording, "Starry Night," spread widely and became the scene's founding myth. Kerbaj has always pushed back against the political weight foreigners load onto it — he resents being treated as an exotic symbol of suffering — but the recording set something in motion regardless. It proved that music made in Beirut, in response to what was actually happening in Beirut, could reach people.

The rock side of the origin story belongs to Scrambled Eggs: Charbel Haber, Tony Elieh, and Marek Rizkallah, who formed in 1998 and mutated across eight records from alt-rock into noise-rock fluent in Faust and Sonic Youth. Their 2006 album Happy Together Filthy Forever, tracked during that same summer's war, is punk under bombardment. They are arguably Lebanon's first post-rock band, and their shadow runs through everything that came after. Julia Sabra of Postcards, years later, described their music as something that "captured what we lived through. It embodies life in Lebanon: this delusional little bubble."

II

If you want to understand why this scene exists, the answer is mostly two people in a studio in Sin el-Fil.

Ziad Nawfal ran the radio show Ruptures on Radio Liban from 1992 to 2021, introducing Lebanese listeners to their own underground. Fadi Tabbal trained as a sound engineer in Montreal, came back to Beirut in 2006, and opened Tunefork Studios in what he describes as an "ugly, brutalist building." They met at La CD-Thèque in 2002, started collaborating, and around 2008 turned their Ruptured Sessions recordings into a label called Ruptured Records.

The label's philosophy was simple: release independent music regardless of whether it would sell. "We cared about the music — despite not knowing whether it would sell or not," Nawfal has said. Tunefork's model is equally unusual. The studio charges no production royalties or fees. Musicians pay only for studio hours, and the in-house team helps arrange and write. Tabbal describes it as being based on the concept that knowledge is free. When the economy collapsed in 2019, the studio shifted to recording free of charge or pay-as-you-can.

Nawfal funded the label out of his own pocket for fifteen years. He received no external grants until after the 2020 port explosion. Tabbal did not take royalties. What they built, essentially, is a subsidy for music that the market would not support — and the music that came out of it is the backbone of the scene.

Charbel Haber's summary is the clearest: "Before Ruptured existed, the Lebanese alternative scene had no home. Now, it does."

III

If you're only going to listen to one band, start here.

Postcards formed in 2012: Julia Sabra on vocals, keys, and guitar; Marwan Tohme on guitar and bass; Pascal Semerdjian on drums. They started playing folk-rock covers in Beirut pubs and slowly found their way toward something denser. Their debut full-length, I'll Be Here in the Morning (2018, Ruptured), arrived braided with dream pop, noise rock, and shoegaze — recorded with Tabbal at Tunefork, released on Ruptured and Germany's T3. Their follow-up, The Good Soldier (2020), landed months before the city came apart, mixing slowcore, surf-rock, and post-punk into something that reads in retrospect like an unintentional premonition.

Then came August 4, 2020. Sabra and Semerdjian were at home near the port when approximately 2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate detonated. The blast killed at least 215 people. Semerdjian was severely injured. Sabra survived because she happened to be standing with her back to a wall.

The album they made afterward, After the Fire, Before the End (2021), is the central document of what this scene has been through. Nine tracks, no self-pity, no distance. Bandcamp premiered it; KEXP premièred "Home Is So Sad" as a piece explicitly about the explosion. It is the record that defined what Postcards are.

IV

The cluster

The scene's real shape isn't a few headline acts — it's a cluster of overlapping projects with largely the same people.

Kinematik is the flagship post-rock band. Anthony Sahyoun and Rudy Ghafari formed it in 2014 as a rock-electronic project that grew into an instrumental group moving between psychedelic improvisation and motorik grooves, with post-rock crescendos throughout. Their debut Ala' (Ruptured, 2017) was tracked to an 8-track reel-to-reel, reportedly the first Lebanese underground act to do so in at least a decade. Think Explosions in the Sky and Can in the same room.

Calamita is the heavier end: Sehnaoui on electric guitar, Tony Elieh (ex-Scrambled Eggs) on bass, and Italian drummer Davide Zolli. Improvisational, loud, closer to free-rock than classic post-rock, but sharing the same build-and-release logic. It is the direct bridge between the old improv guard and the younger bands.

Snakeskin, the duo of Julia Sabra and Fadi Tabbal, is what happens when shoegaze dissolves into ambient. Their 2022 self-titled record uses tape loops, synths, vocals, and drum machines to build something moody and industrial — described by both artists as a reflection on home in a place where "there is no rebirth, no renewal." It was born after the explosion.

Sanam is six people: Sandy Chamoun, Anthony Sahyoun, Pascal Semerdjian, Marwan Tohme, Farah Kaddour on buzuq, and Antonio Hajj Moussa. They came together in 2021 for a one-off Irtijal performance with Hans Joachim Irmler of Faust, then stayed together. Their debut Aykathani Malakon sets centuries-old Arabic poetry against free-psych and post-rock, recorded in a traditional house in the mountain village of Saqi Reshmaya. They now play We Out Here in England and Le Guess Who? in Utrecht.

Two or the Dragon is Abed Kobeissy on buzuq and effects, and Ali Hout on Arabic and Persian percussion. Their album Dance Grooves for the Weary (Ruptured) was made after the 2020 explosion, weaving processed buzuq with field recordings of Beirut — including paperclips. Kobeissy, speaking about the broader ecosystem, put the situation plainly: Ruptured, Tunefork, and Irtijal are "doing amazing work, especially in the absence of any government support. If anything, one could argue that the government is working against us."

V

For a few years after the explosion, starting a new band in Beirut seemed close to absurd. Then a few people did it anyway.

GLIDDEEE began in 2022 when Marco Bazerji and Chris started jamming in a university room, working out their sound as they went. When they found the rest of their bandmates, something clicked. Their EP — two and a half years in the making, through drafts and creative arguments and a war — landed in 2024 with six songs ranging from noise-punk to post-rock to something harder to name. The references are Unwound, Slint, Number Girl, Sparklehorse, Phil Elvrum. But the song INHALER, which started as a piece about Bazerji's fight with pneumonia in 2023, gradually became something else: a reflection on six years of conflict in Lebanon and Palestine, on the impossibility of breathing in a country that keeps collapsing.

Bazerji is now in London for a master's degree, and he finds the displacement hard. "Alone I don't get the drive I used to have when playing back in Beirut," he says. "The sense of community and the creativity we have in Lebanon can't be replicated anywhere else."

Bliss formed in 2022 when Julie Abou Kasm, Rebal Abu Fadel, and Bob (later replaced by Andrew Kareh) started writing originals at AUB's music club. Julie had never sung before. The scene felt like a desert to them — plenty of solo artists and DJs, but hardly anyone their age in a band playing original music. Then they played their first show at Quadrangle and saw who came out, and something shifted. They dug into the history: Scrambled Eggs, Postcards, The Wanton Bishops. They understood what the city had made before them.

Their debut EP, You Should Quit Your Job and Play Outside (With Me) (2024), is grunge-shoegaze rooted in teenage heartbreak — raw, direct, and unashamed of it. Their second album, due in November 2025, goes somewhere different. When the March 2025 war escalation came, Julie found herself sitting on her balcony trying to write, and for the first time she wasn't thinking about a past relationship. She was thinking about the world outside. She's originally from South Lebanon. Watching the Litani River — somewhere she used to picnic — become a front line produced one of the new album's central songs, "Sauded."

"Being with Bliss has helped my self esteem flourish," she says. "Looking back over the last two years, I'm a completely different person, but I'm someone I've dreamt of becoming mostly because of this band."

This summer, they're playing eight cities in Europe in sixteen days.

Singer-songwriter Michel Maouad sits at a slightly different angle — closer to the Americana and slacker-rock tradition, influenced by MJ Lenderman, Big Thief, Jason Molina, Silver Jews. His album Good Thing We Cleaned The Stairs draws on that world's plain-spoken directness. He says Postcards made him want to become a better musician, and Karl Mattar (of Interbellum) made him want to write better songs. "My music is mostly about me as a person and isn't directly political," he says simply. "Every song I ever wrote came directly from personal experience — women I loved, jobs I hated, procrastination."

VI

What holds it together

The scene has lost a lot. The economy collapsed in 2019 and never recovered; the lira lost most of its value, banks froze savings, and a fifth of Lebanon's doctors left within two years of the collapse. The neighborhoods hardest hit by the 2020 explosion — Mar Mikhael, Gemmayzeh, Karantina — are precisely where the music lives. In the 2024 war, Beirut & Beyond canceled its festival edition and Tunefork organized fundraising for displaced families. The pattern is consistent: catastrophe, then the same small network organizing relief, and eventually new records.

What holds it together is partly structural. A free studio, a stubborn label, a festival that has run for over twenty years, a rehearsal space in a depot under a building that Bliss, GLIDDEEE, Boond, and Ta2reeban share and call "TuneSpoon." Mayssa Jallad, who has released with Ruptured since 2016, says of the label: "We're like family at this point."

But it is also the density of the connections. The same dozen people play in four different projects, produce each other's records, lend each other pedals and cables, pass gigs along when they don't fit. The bands that Bliss name as local favorites — Postcards, Snakeskin, Boond, Ta2reeban, Sourgrass — are the same bands they share stages and rehearsal space with. Marco Bazerji names essentially the same constellation from London, missing it.

The diaspora question is real and unresolved. Bands that can tour Europe — Postcards in the UK, France, Portugal, and Germany; Sanam at Le Guess Who? — are sustaining themselves in ways the local economy no longer allows. And when someone leaves, as Bazerji did for London, the hole is felt. "What disappears when someone emigrates — not emotionally, but practically, for the music?" is one of the honest questions this scene lives with.

For now, the records keep coming. Snakeskin in 2022. Sanam's debut in 2023. GLIDDEEE's EP in 2024. Bliss's second album coming in late 2025. Somewhere in Beirut right now, someone is probably demoing something in a rehearsal space they share with five other bands, paying almost nothing, figuring it out as they go.

VII

Where to start

Postcards — After the Fire, Before the End (T3/Ruptured, 2021). Start with "Home Is So Sad."

Postcards — I'll Be Here in the Morning (Ruptured/T3, 2018). The first full-length; try "Bright Lights."

Kinematik — Ala' (Ruptured, 2017). Instrumental post-rock on reel-to-reel tape.

Snakeskin — Snakeskin (Ruptured/Beacon Sound, 2022). Shoegaze drifting into ambient industrial.

Sanam — Aykathani Malakon (2023). Arabic poetry, free-psych, post-rock, a house in the mountains.

Two or the Dragon — Dance Grooves for the Weary (Ruptured). Processed buzuq and Beirut field recordings.

Scrambled Eggs — Happy Together Filthy Forever (Ruptured/Incognito, 2006). The origin point; punk-noise under bombardment.

GLIDDEEE — EP (2024). The new noise.

Bliss — You Should Quit Your Job and Play Outside (With Me) EP (2024). Grunge-shoegaze from the generation that came after the explosion.

Mazen Kerbaj — Starry Night (2006). Not pretty. Not optional.
